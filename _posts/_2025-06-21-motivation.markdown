I'm adapting much of this post from a draft I had written over a year ago, around when I was recovering from pretty bad burnout. And now, I return to this idea after finishing my Master's degree at Stanford, feeling a bit burnt out once more. In my last post (over a year ago...), I wrote that burnout is a consequence of burning these resources, and not getting the requisite sense of fulfillment. The more interesting question to pose is why I feel particularly suscpetible to burnout?

As a person, I index very heavily on a sense of purpose. To slightly rephrase something I previously wrote and incorrectly borrow the 80/20 rule, I generally subscribe to giving your first priority 80% of your time, and allocating the remaining central aspect of my self-characterization is the notion that if I really want something--if I'm chasing my life's purpose--I will definitely get it, by any means necessary--through burning any amount of effort, time, and rest. 

Of course, I think the real naivete in my  is the preconditiion that you can do exactly want you want. Burnout is a mirror of a mismatch of mission and motivation; of actions and needs. The even more difficult precondition is that you really know what you want

 When trying to better understand my burnout, the question I had instead been asking the people around me was, "do you wake up looking forward to doing your work?" (for the record, my own answer at the time had been **no**) Of the responses I got, there are two I distinctly remember, from which I will tell two different perspectives:

__"Maybe you need to mentally toughen up a bit" (or: on intrinsic motivations)__: While my perseverance can be an issue ([I tend to work in bursts of energy](https://twitter.com/harriszainul/status/1250071517007876099)), I know this claim to be false simply based off of the fact that, for projects that I found more inherently interesting, I worked through the roadblocks.

\
My personal theory behind motivation, borrowing language from unsupervised RL, is that most people have a ranked set of intrinsic rewards that they optimize for, in addition to the rewards specific to a task. More importantly, the amount of effort corresponding to a task is superlinear to the priority given (for example, something like 80% of your capacity for the top 20% of your priorities). Thus, just as intrinsic reward functions in RL encourage exploration, I think that when the extrinsic reward of what you're working on is low, people are naturally encouraged to consider different options, and exert less effort in their current task. Put simply in English: if you don't care about what you're doing, you're not going to put in the effort to do it well.

I feel that there are several dichotomoies at play: extrinsic and intrinsic motivation; process and results; work-life balance and integration. Of these two "competing" perspectives, I find the set of the former statements to be somewhat pessimistic: it places value primarily on the nature of the work, and ignores the human elements of personal growth and Adler's "community feeling". 


Lord knows I didn't push through my 162 projects because I loved the content (to be fair, I did find the projects interesting), nor did I push through intense burnout as president of a college organization because I loved the power that came with it (quite the opposite). Rather, it was something much more basic: an addiction to the process of continually scoping and solving problems, and feelings of gratitude and responsiblity to the people around me. There are also entire fields of study dedicated to the science of motivation--how to coach and place people in positions to succeed, which I have only scratched the surface of and want to push further into.

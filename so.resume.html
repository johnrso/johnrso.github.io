Work Experience

      In my time in the Dublin Engineering and Design Academy, I have had many opportunities to see engineering in a real world setting. In February 2018, I went on a field trip to Cisco. At Cisco, the tour guides offered us a glimpse of the work culture there, the technology they are creating, and the campus itself. There were many demos of their products. For example, one tool they showed us was a network which could remotely control various settings of a city, such as lighting and humidity. In April 2019, I toured the wastewater treatment facility at the Dublin San Ramon Services District. At the facility, we walked through the process of treating water riddled with sediments, bacteria, and other waste to be safe for consumption. In addition to the facilities themselves, we also toured their research lab and learned about the extent to which the facility affects California, processing millions of gallons per day and delivering water as far as San Leandro.


      Through the various engineering classes I've taken over the years, I've had the chance to listen to several industry speakers. James Moorehead, a product manager at Google, relayed his entry into the computer science field, the interview process for a company as influential as Google, and the skills he uses daily. A product manager needs to be able to thrive in chaos, and guide a group of engineers to a full realization of a product. He showed us his brainchild, Google Bulletin, an app designed to provide a platform for local stories, and discussed the development cycles of an application. Laura Kegelmeyer worked with machine learning and computer vision within the Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory to monitor the operation and maintenance of the NIF's massive laser. She covered how the laser works mechanically and the process used to maintain it; using deep learning, she is able to detect damages on the lenses caused by the energy emitted. I found it interesting how she was able to apply machine learning algorithms, something I had experimented with over the summer, to autonomously monitor the health of the system to such a high degree of accuracy.


      In junior year, the DEDA Mentorship program provided me with the opportunity to work with an industry mentor. I was paired with Alex Montano, a data architect at Intel. Under his guidance, we met once a month to keep track of long term and short term goals, talk about the college process, and even did mock interviews to prepare for a real world one. As a younger mentor, Mr. Montano was able to relay a relatable, more modern view of someone entering the industry, and provided me with new perspectives on the field of engineering and the skills necessary to succeed. Under his mentorship, I refined my ability to set SMART goals, conduct interviews, and set myself up to enter the industry post-college. The mentorship experience provided me with guidance and advice to pursue engineering further in the future.
